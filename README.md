##  Summary

This report analyses daily personnel embarkation activity across offshore oil & gas embarkation points in Nigeria. It covers a 4-month operational window, tracking **193,871 personnel movements** across more than 10 embarkation points, with data on government agency clearances, swipe records, compliance flags, and flight activity. The dominant hub is **BRPH – Bristow PH**, which accounts for the majority of all traffic, while **September 2021** stands out as the peak operational month.

---

## Key Insights

### 1. Bristow PH Dominates All Activity
BRPH – Bristow PH handled **114,166 embarked personnel** — nearly **59% of total volume** — and led both outbound (66,169) and inbound (47,329) swipe counts by a wide margin. No other point comes close, making it a single point of operational dependency.

### 2. September Was the Busiest Month
Personnel embarked peaked in **September at 57,584**, representing a **28% increase** over August and nearly **3.4x July's volume**. Activity then declined steadily into November (26,039), suggesting a seasonal or project-driven surge mid-period.

### 3. Significant Compliance Gaps
Out of total personnel processed:
- **2,175** had an **inactive OSP** (Offshore Safety Permit)
- **841** had **no physical card**
- **351** had **no OSP at all**

Combined, over **3,300 personnel** moved through embarkation points with some form of compliance issue — a notable safety and regulatory risk.

### 4. NIG Customs Leads Government Agency Clearances
NIG Customs cleared **19,376 personnel**, followed by NIS (13,489) and PHS (4,923). Smaller agencies like NOSDRA (1,341) and Nigerian Navy (1,891) played supporting roles, reflecting the multi-agency nature of offshore access control.

### 5. Swipe-to-Embark Gap
Total swiped (**190,787**) vs total embarked (**193,871**) shows a gap of ~3,084 records. This discrepancy may indicate missed swipes, system errors, or manual overrides — and warrants further investigation.

### 6. Centralised Clearance Is a Bottleneck Risk
**Lagos Office alone cleared 22,771 personnel** — nearly 3x the next individual (Emiko Gula at 7,640). Heavy reliance on a single office or person for clearances creates a bottleneck and continuity risk.

## Recommendations

| # | Recommendation | Priority |
|---|---------------|----------|
| 1 | **Enforce OSP compliance gates** — Block embarkation for personnel flagged with inactive or missing OSPs before they reach the boarding point. | 🔴 High |
| 2 | **Investigate the swipe-embark discrepancy** — Audit the ~3,084 unmatched records to identify system failures or manual bypasses. | 🔴 High |
| 3 | **Distribute clearance responsibilities** — Reduce over-reliance on Lagos Office by empowering regional clearance officers at high-volume points. | 🟠 Medium |
| 4 | **Capacity planning for September peaks** — Analyse what drives the Q3 surge and pre-position resources (staff, equipment, systems) ahead of the next cycle. | 🟠 Medium |
| 5 | **Strengthen secondary embarkation points** — ESBL and QIEK handle meaningful volumes (26K and 23K) but receive far fewer resources relative to BRPH. Assess whether infrastructure matches demand. | 🟡 Low |
| 6 | **Automate physical card verification** — Integrate card scan validation into the swipe system to eliminate the 841 no-card cases at source. | 🟡 Low |
